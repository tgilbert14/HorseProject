# Horse Selector — User Validation Kit

*A ready-to-run plan to put the app in front of ~10–15 real first-time / syndicate
owners and learn whether the pivot actually works — before investing in more build.*

The viability study concluded **viable-with-pivot**: this app only works as a free,
transparent *education & due-diligence notebook* for novices, not a BUY/PASS oracle for
pros. That conclusion is a **hypothesis about people.** This kit tests it cheaply.

You can run the whole thing in about **two weeks, part-time, for ~$150 in incentives.**

> 🖨 **Print-and-fill field pack:** a ready-to-print PDF of the moderator sheet, a fillable
> per-participant log, and the participant survey is in
> **[`user-validation-field-pack.pdf`](./user-validation-field-pack.pdf)** (source:
> `user-validation-field-pack.html`). Print one log sheet per participant.

## 1. The one question this test answers

> **When a genuinely-new owner uses Horse Selector, do they come away feeling more
> confident and competent — or more confused (or, worse, falsely confident)?**

Everything else is a sub-question of that.

## 2. The assumptions we're actually testing

These come straight from the "must be true" list in `docs/VIABILITY.md`. Each maps to
something you can watch a real person do.

| # | Assumption (must be true) | What to watch for |
|---|---|---|
| A1 | The novice *wants to understand* a horse, not just passively hold a share | Do they lean in on the guided read & legends, or bounce? |
| A2 | The **teaching checklist** builds literacy (they learn *what to look at*) | Can they explain a pillar back to you afterward, unprompted? |
| A3 | The honesty framing lands — they read the score as a *lean, not a verdict* | Do they over-trust the number, or repeat back "it's just my inputs"? |
| A4 | The **price sanity check** feels genuinely useful ("is this price sane?") | Do they react — "oh, that's actually helpful"? Do they get the book nuance? |
| A5 | Data entry isn't so heavy they abandon before finishing one horse | Where do they stall, sigh, or give up? |
| A6 | The **due-diligence report** is something they'd actually bring to an agent/vet | Would they send it? To whom? Does it make them feel prepared? |

If A2, A3, and A6 hold, the pivot is real. If A5 fails hard, fix friction first.

## 3. Who to recruit (and where)

**Target:** someone who owns (or is seriously considering) a **fractional / syndicate
share** or a first small racehorse, and has **little or no** evaluation experience.
Explicitly **not** bloodstock agents, trainers, or experienced buyers — they are not the user.

**Where to find ~12 of them:**
- MyRacehorse / Commonwealth / other micro-share owner communities & their social groups
- Racing fan forums and subreddits (r/horseracing), first-owner Facebook groups
- Friends-of-friends who've said "I'd love to own a racehorse someday"
- Local racing clubs / ownership open days

**Screener (keep or drop in 30 seconds):**
1. Have you ever owned a share in, or bought, a racehorse? *(any = keep; a lot = drop — too expert)*
2. Have you ever evaluated a horse to buy, or worked in bloodstock? *(yes/professionally = drop)*
3. On a scale of 1–5, how confident are you reading a sales-catalogue page? *(1–3 = ideal)*

**Recruiting message (copy-paste):**

> Hi — I'm testing a free tool that helps first-time racehorse owners understand a horse
> before they buy or buy into a share. Looking for people who are *new* to this (that's the
> whole point). It's a 30-minute video call where you try it and think out loud — no prep,
> no right answers. $10–15 gift card as a thank-you. Interested?

## 4. Logistics

- **Format:** 30 min, 1:1, video call with screen share (Zoom/Meet/Teams). Remote is fine.
- **Sample:** 10–15 sessions. Patterns are usually obvious by ~8; stop when you stop learning.
- **Incentive:** $10–15 gift card each (~$150 total).
- **Record** (with consent) so you can rewatch the stuck moments.
- **One moderator, one silent note-taker** if you can; solo is workable.

## 5. The session protocol (30 min)

**(0–4 min) Warm-up — set them at ease.**
- "There are no wrong answers — I'm testing the tool, not you."
- "Please think out loud: say what you're looking at, what you expect, what's confusing."
- Context Qs: "Tell me about your interest in owning a racehorse. Have you ever looked at
  a horse to buy? How did that feel?"

**(4–24 min) Five tasks — hand them the URL, then mostly stay quiet.**

> **T1 — Learn (cold open).** "Spend two minutes getting a feel for this. What do you think
> it's for?" *(Tests first impression & positioning.)*
>
> **T2 — Legends.** "Use it to figure out what makes a *great* racehorse's page great.
> Tell me one thing you learned." *(Tests A2 — does it teach?)*
>
> **T3 — Evaluate.** "A friend offers you a share in a yearling. Load a famous horse as a
> stand-in, or enter one, and tell me: does this look like a good horse, and how sure are
> you?" *(Tests A2/A3 — do they read the score honestly? Do they over-trust it?)*
>
> **T4 — Price.** "It's for sale at Keeneland September for $60,000. Is that a normal price?
> Now imagine it's in Book 1 instead." *(Tests A4 — do they get the sanity check and the
> book nuance?)*
>
> **T5 — Take it to your agent.** "You want a second opinion from an expert. Get something
> you could send them." *(Tests A6 — do they find & value the report?)*

**(24–30 min) Debrief — the money questions.**
- "In one sentence, what is this for?" *(Did the positioning land?)*
- "What would you tell a friend who's about to buy their first share?"
- "Did anything make you trust it more, or less?"
- "What was the most confusing or annoying moment?"
- "Would you use this again before a real purchase? Why / why not?"

## 6. Post-session survey (2 min, send afterward)

Rate 1–5 (5 = strongly agree):
1. I understand racehorses better than before I used this.
2. I know *what to look at* when evaluating a horse now.
3. I trusted what the tool told me. *(You want ~3–4 here — high trust + healthy skepticism.
   A 5 from someone who over-trusted the number is a **red flag**, not a win.)*
4. The price check was useful.
5. I would use this before a real purchase.
6. **0–10:** How likely are you to recommend this to another new owner? *(NPS)*

Plus one open text: *"The one thing that would make this more useful is ___."*

## 7. Decision rules (in the spirit of the app itself)

**Green — keep building the notebook, then chase distribution:**
- ≥60% can explain a pillar back unprompted (A2), *and*
- Most read the score as a lean, not a verdict (A3), *and*
- ≥50% say they'd use it again (survey Q5 ≥ 4), *and*
- Several volunteer the report or price check as the best part.

**Yellow — the idea works but the execution doesn't. Fix, then re-test:**
- People value it but consistently stall on data entry (A5) → cut required fields / add a fast path.
- They get the pillars but miss the report/price features → fix discoverability.

**Red — reconsider the pivot itself:**
- They finish and still feel *no more competent* (A2 fails), or
- They act on the score as if it were a verdict despite the honesty framing (A3 fails —
  the false-precision risk is real and the score framing needs to go further), or
- Nobody would use it again and nobody would send the report (A5/A6 fail).

## 8. Turning findings into the next build

1. After each session, write **3 bullets: biggest struggle, biggest delight, one surprising quote.**
2. After ~8 sessions, cluster the struggles. The **most-repeated struggle is your next ticket** —
   don't average, don't cherry-pick.
3. Map each fix to a phase in `docs/VIABILITY.md` (friction → Phase 1 field-reduction; trust →
   Phase 0 framing; value → Phase 2 price data; distribution → Phase 3).
4. Re-test the *same tasks* after the fix. Convergence on the same tasks is your signal you're done.
