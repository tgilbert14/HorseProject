3) Performance Potential / Proof

9–10: elite class/speed profile or proven top-level performer

7–8: strong figures, consistent results, clear upside

5–6: moderate profile, unclear ceiling

1–4: limited ability shown, weak indicators

4) Price-Value Edge

9–10: clear under-market opportunity, high upside per dollar

7–8: fair price with some positive edge

5–6: market-correct pricing, no major edge

1–4: expensive for risk/quality profile

5) Mindset & Trainability

9–10: professional attitude, focused, highly trainable

7–8: mostly good behavior, manageable quirks

5–6: average attitude, inconsistent focus

1–4: difficult temperament, reliability concerns

Selection Guide — What the factors mean

Pedigree (≈ 30% of score)

  • Sire AEI / CI — Average Earnings Index measures a sire's progeny earnings against the average (1.0). Elite sires consistently exceed 2.0. CI compares against the mares he's covered.
  • Dam quality — A productive dam (multiple winners, black-type) is statistically the strongest single predictor in many studies. Look at the second dam too.
  • Black-type within 3 generations — Italicized/bolded names in the catalogue. The closer up, the better.
  • Nicking — Statistical compatibility of sire line × broodmare sire line. A+ nicks have produced disproportionate stakes winners.
  • Dosage Index — Speed-vs-stamina balance derived from chef-de-race ancestors. ~2–4 favors speed, <1 favors stamina/turf.
  • Inbreeding — Light inbreeding to superior ancestors (3×4, 4×4) can concentrate quality; heavy inbreeding (>6%) raises risk.

Conformation (≈ 20%)

  • Balance — Shoulder length ≈ hip length; horse should look "in three equal parts."
  • Shoulder — A long, sloping shoulder (~45°) gives stride length and absorbs concussion.
  • Hindquarter — The engine. Look for muscle, length from hip to hock, and a well-let-down hock.
  • Cannon bone — Short, dense, and substantial. ≥8" circumference for a yearling colt is a common benchmark.
  • Pasterns — 45–50°. Too upright = concussion injuries; too sloped = soft-tissue strain.
  • Knees — Should be flat from the side, not "back at the knee" (predisposes to chips) or "over at the knee."
  • Walk — A free, overstepping walk strongly correlates with athletic gallop.

Veterinary (≈ 15%)

  • Repository X-rays at major sales — review with a vet. OCD lesions in stifles/hocks are red flags.
  • Throat scope — Laryngeal Grade I–II is standard; Grade III/IV signals roaring risk.
  • Heart, wind, eyes — basic soundness checks.

Performance (raced horses)

  • Earnings, speed figures (Beyer/Timeform), highest class won, and soundness history.
  • For unraced yearlings/weanlings this section is skipped automatically.

Auction / Value

  • Price sanity check (recommended). In the Auction & Price card, pick the sale (and book) your horse is in. The app compares your reserve to that sale's real published median and tells you whether the price is below, within, or above the normal range for that sale — grounded in actual market data, not a formula. It answers "is this price normal for this sale?", not "is it right for this horse" (a standout is worth well above the median; a plain one, well below).
  • The older "Value Score" on the list view = Overall Score ÷ price (in $10k units) — a rough quality-per-dollar ratio. It surfaces cheaper horses whose score outpaces their reserve, but note it structurally favors the lowest-priced horse and is not anchored to comparable sale prices. Prefer the price sanity check above.

Breeding

  • Mare: produce record > her own race record. An older mare with multiple winners is more valuable than a young one with no foals.
  • Stallion: % stakes winners from runners is the cleanest quality metric; book size and stud fee indicate market belief.
  • Cross / nick: switch the mode toggle to a breeding mode and the cross/nick fields will be weighted heavily.

Five Pillars Workflow (Recommended)

  • Score each pillar 1–10 using the rubric in the Five Pillars card.
  • The score rolls up the data YOU entered. Blank categories are excluded, not counted as zero — so a sparse record can still show a high number off very little information.
  • Interpret the on-paper read as a lean, not a verdict: it flags where a page looks strong, average, or weak so you know what to dig into with your agent and vet — it is not a bid instruction.
  • Watch "Data filled": it tells you how many of the 9 categories you've populated. It is a completeness gauge for your own inputs, not a probability that the horse will run well.

How to read the score honestly

  • No score predicts a racehorse. In published work, even the single best signal available at the yearling stage — the sale price itself, which already bakes in every agent's and vet's opinion — correlates with later race ratings at only about r≈0.36 (explaining ~13% of the variation). Racing ability is also only weakly heritable. A hand-built score cannot beat that ceiling, so treat any number here as a way to organize your read, never as a forecast.
  • The "Value Score" is a rough quality-per-dollar ratio, not true value. Because it divides the score by price, the cheaper of two similar horses always ranks higher — it does not compare price to what a horse like this should cost. Use it to surface cheap horses worth a second look, not as a statement that a horse is underpriced.
  • Presets are a learning benchmark. Secretariat, Frankel and Winx score high because their elite race records are entered for them. Seeing your prospect near an all-time great means your inputs resemble theirs on the fields provided — it is a teaching reference, not a claim your horse is in their class.

This tool is a decision aid — a structured notebook for organizing your own judgement — not a predictive model and not a substitute for a qualified bloodstock agent and veterinarian. Always inspect and vet in person before bidding.

Glossary — plain English

Every bit of jargon on this form, explained simply

Pedigree & breeding

Sire / Dam / Damsire
The father, the mother, and the mother's father. The damsire matters because the female family is often the most reliable part of a pedigree.
AEI — Average Earnings Index
How much a sire's foals earn compared with an average horse. 1.0 = average, 2.0+ = elite. It's an earnings ratio, so it's inflated by big-money opportunities — a clue, not gospel.
CI — Comparable Index
What the sire's mares were already producing before him. Reading AEI next to CI tells you how much credit the sire really deserves.
Black-type
Bold/italic names in a sales catalogue — relatives who won or placed in stakes (the best) races. The closer up the page, the stronger the family.
Nick / nicking
How well a sire's bloodline has historically "clicked" with the damsire's bloodline, graded A+ down to F. Some crosses produce stakes winners far more often than chance.
Dosage Index
A rough speed-vs-stamina number from a horse's key ancestors. ~2–4 leans speed, under 1 leans stamina/turf. Contested and distance-oriented — treat lightly.
Inbreeding
The same superior ancestor appearing on both sides (e.g. "3×4"). A little can concentrate quality; a lot raises risk.
Produce record
For a mare: the foals she's had and how they ran. A proven producer usually outweighs her own race record.
Stud fee / Book size
What a stallion costs to breed to, and how many mares he covers a year. Both are the market's live vote on how good he is.
Foaling date
Every Northern-Hemisphere thoroughbred shares a January 1 birthday, so a February foal is physically ahead of a May foal early in their careers.

On the track

Starts / Wins / Places
Races run, races won, and times finishing 2nd or 3rd. Win-and-place rate matters more than raw totals.
Speed figure (Beyer / Timeform)
A single number rating how fast a performance actually was, adjusted for track and conditions. ~100 is Grade 1 class; 118+ is historic.
Class (Grade / Group 1–3, Listed)
The level of race. Grade/Group 1 is the top; then G2, G3, Listed, then handicaps/allowances, then maidens (for horses yet to win).

The horse & the vet

Conformation
The horse's physical build — how its frame, limbs and angles are put together. Related to athletic ability, but only weakly, so don't over-weight it.
The walk
A free, ground-covering, over-stepping walk is one of the better physical clues that a horse can gallop. Watch it at the barn.
Repository / x-rays
A set of pre-sale x-rays (and scopes) the sale company holds. Your own vet reviews them — a serious finding is a hard reason to walk away.
Scope / laryngeal grade / "wind" / roaring
A camera check of the throat. Grades III–IV suggest a breathing (airway) problem that can limit a horse — sometimes called "roaring".
OCD
A joint/cartilage flaw (osteochondrosis) that shows on x-rays. Some are minor; some need surgery.
Cannon / pastern / hock
Lower-leg parts vets and buyers scrutinise: the cannon should be short and dense, pasterns angled ~45–50°, the hock (the "knee" of the hind leg) strong and correct.

At the sale

Hip / Lot number
The horse's number in the catalogue and the order it sells in — the sticker is literally on its hip.
Reserve / RNA
The minimum the seller will accept. "RNA" (Reserve Not Attained) means it didn't reach that and stayed unsold.
Consignor
The seller/agent presenting the horse at the sale. A top consignor's prep and honesty carry weight.
Pinhook
Buying a young horse to resell at a profit later (e.g. a weanling to sell as a yearling). A speculative business — fewer than half turn a profit.
Value Score (this app)
Overall score ÷ price — a rough quality-per-dollar ratio. Handy for surfacing cheaper horses, but it structurally favours the lowest price, so sanity-check it against real sale prices.
Price sanity check (this app)
Compares your reserve to the real published median for the sale you pick, and tells you whether the price is below, within, or above the normal range for that sale. Grounded in actual market data — it's about the sale, not a verdict on the individual horse.
